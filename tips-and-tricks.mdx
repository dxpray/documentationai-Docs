Get the most out of Rose and the search tools with these best practices.

## Rose AI Search Tips

**Be specific, but conversational.** Rose responds well to natural language that describes the role clearly. Include seniority, location, industry, and any non-negotiables upfront.

> Good: *"I need a founding AE with 4+ years B2B SaaS experience, based in London or remote UK"*
>
> Less effective: *"sales person"*

**Use follow-up prompts to refine.** If Rose's first packages aren't quite right, continue the conversation — tell her what you liked and what to change. She'll iterate on her results.

**Review multiple packages.** Rose often returns two or more candidate packages with different interpretations of your brief. Each represents a different candidate profile worth considering.

**Check the reserve.** Each package shows candidates "in reserve" beyond the initial 25. If the first batch doesn't produce enough interviews, ask Rose to show you more from the same package.

## Manual Filter Tips

**Start broad, then narrow.** Begin with one or two filters (e.g. role title and location) and watch how the match count changes as you add more. Adding all filters at once can result in a pool that's too small to be useful.

**Prioritise essential criteria first.** Focus on the must-haves (geography, job title) before adding nice-to-haves (specific skills, education). This helps you understand the size of the core market before constraining it.

**Use the Company filter strategically.** Targeting candidates from specific companies is one of the most reliable signals of relevant experience. If you're hiring for a specific vertical, compile a list of the leading companies in that space and enter them into the Company filter. Candidates from those firms are likely to have exactly the background you need.

**Use the engagement signals.** The "Recently changed jobs", "Posts on LinkedIn", and "In the news" toggles in the Skills & Keywords filter can significantly improve response rates — candidates who are active or have recently moved roles are often more open to new opportunities.

**Adapt to your industry.** Some industries use specific job titles, accreditations, or keywords that don't appear in profiles from other sectors. Bear this in mind when configuring your filters — a keyword that's standard in one field may not appear in another.

## Boolean Search

The Skills & Keywords and Role & Title filters support Boolean search operators. These allow you to build more precise, flexible queries.

**Simple searches — no brackets needed.** When searching within a single category, list terms with OR between them:

```text
VP OR "vice president" OR EVP OR SVP
```

```text
sales OR AE OR "account executive" OR BD OR "business development"
```

**Use quotation marks for multi-word phrases.** Without quotes, each word is treated independently. Use quotes to search for an exact phrase:

```text
✓  AE OR "account executive"
✗  AE OR account executive   (this searches for AE, or account, or executive — not the same thing)
```

**Use brackets when combining two categories.** Wrap each group in brackets and join with AND:

```text
(manager) AND (sales OR account OR BD OR "business development")
```

```text
(manager OR lead OR senior) AND (sales OR account OR BD OR "business development")
```

This surfaces results like *sales lead*, *BD manager*, or *senior account* — combinations you might not have typed individually.

**The same logic applies to skills searches:**

```text
("javascript" OR JS) AND (python OR python3)
```
